# Heat Alert Fan Controller — Analog Temperature Monitor with Automatic Cooling

A three-zone temperature monitor with automatic fan actuation, built entirely from discrete analog components. An NTC thermistor feeds the sensing divider; two LM311 open-collector comparators partition the sensed voltage into low, medium, and high bands; a hardware NOR network implemented in BJTs gates the medium-state LED so only one indicator lights at a time; a 555 astable produces the ~1 Hz alert blink pattern and the fan-enable signal; and an IRLZ44N logic-level N-MOSFET switches a 12 V fan from an isolated rail. The design target is Multisim 14 for simulation, with the NTC thermistor stood in by a potentiometer during resistance sweeps.

## Features

- Three-zone temperature indication — yellow (safe), steady red (moderate), blinking red + fan (alert)
- Two independent LM311 comparators resolving the low / medium and medium / high thresholds
- Hardware NOR built from two 2N2222A pull-down transistors sharing a single pull-up resistor — gates the medium-state LED so it activates only when both the low and high paths are inactive
- 555 timer in astable configuration generating both the alert blink pattern and the fan-enable signal from a single oscillator
- Logic-level IRLZ44N N-MOSFET driving the 12 V fan load through a low-side switch with gate pull-down for fail-safe power-down behaviour
- Potentiometer substitution for the thermistor in simulation, allowing manual sweep through all three operating bands

## Circuit Overview

### Supply rails

Control circuitry runs from **VCC = 10 V** — LM311 comparators, the 555, every BJT stage, and all three LEDs. The fan load runs from a separate **VEE = 12 V** rail. The two supplies share a common ground, and every comparator decision, LED current, and transistor emitter/source references that same node. The single-ground topology is what keeps the threshold comparisons meaningful — if the reference dividers and V_sense did not sit on a common 0 V, the comparator outputs would drift with rail noise.

### Sensing and reference network

The thermistor (or its simulation stand-in, the potentiometer `R8`) forms a divider between VCC and ground. The divider output, `V_sense`, is the quantity the comparators observe. As temperature rises, thermistor resistance drops and `V_sense` climbs:

| Temperature band | `V_sense` |
|------------------|-----------|
| Low | ≈ 0 V |
| Medium | ≈ 4 V |
| High | ≈ 8 V |

Two fixed voltages set the band boundaries:

- `V_low` ≈ 2 V — low / medium threshold, generated by **R10 (8 kΩ)** and **R2 (2 kΩ)** as a 10 V × 2 / 10 divider
- `V_high` ≈ 5 V — medium / high threshold, generated by **R1 (10 kΩ)** and **R4 (10 kΩ)** as a 10 V × 1 / 2 divider

All divider bottoms sit at ground, keeping every sensed and reference voltage on the same reference axis.

### Low / medium comparator and LED logic

Comparator **U1 (LM311)** resolves the low threshold — `V_sense` on one input, `V_low` on the other. The LM311 is open-collector; **R9 (3 kΩ)** pulls the output up to VCC when the internal transistor is off and the output node floats free. When the internal transistor conducts, the node is pulled hard to ground.

The U1 output feeds two BJT chains with opposite polarity intent:

- **Q3, Q5 → LED1 (yellow)** activates in the low band (`V_sense < V_low`)
- **Q4, Q2 → LED2 (red, steady)** activates in the medium band — but only when the high-temperature path is also inactive

That last qualifier is the reason the medium LED needs the hardware NOR described below.

### Hardware NOR around NODE_M

The medium state is defined as "not low and not high." Rather than reaching for a discrete logic IC, the design realizes this NOR with two 2N2222A pull-down transistors and a single 1 kΩ pull-up:

- Input **A** — the low-state signal, drives **Q5**'s base through **R15 (2 kΩ)**
- Input **B** — the high-state signal, drives **Q4**'s base through **R7 (2 kΩ)**
- Both collectors tie together at `NODE_M`, which is held HIGH by **R5 (1 kΩ)** to VCC
- `NODE_M` feeds the collector of **Q2** and the anode of **LED2**

The behaviour of `NODE_M` follows directly from wired-OR logic on the pull-down side:

- If A is asserted, Q5 conducts and `NODE_M` is clamped to ground through its collector–emitter path
- If B is asserted, Q4 conducts and does the same
- If either or both are asserted, at least one transistor is on and `NODE_M` stays LOW
- Only when both A and B are deasserted are Q5 and Q4 both off, the pull-down paths open, and R5 drags `NODE_M` up to VCC

The logic expression is `NODE_M = ¬(A ∨ B)`, which is the NOR truth table built entirely in discrete silicon. With `NODE_M` LOW, Q2 has no collector supply and LED2 cannot light regardless of what Q2's base is doing. With `NODE_M` HIGH, Q2 gates LED2 in the usual low-side switch configuration.

### High-temperature comparator and 555 alert

Comparator **U4 (LM311)** resolves the high threshold by comparing `V_sense` against `V_high`. Its open-collector output uses **R3 (3 kΩ)** as the pull-up. When `V_sense > V_high`, the output releases, R3 drives it HIGH, and **Q1** saturates through its base resistor **R16 (2 kΩ)**. Q1 enables the **LM555CN (U2)** in astable configuration.

The 555 timing network uses **R12, R13, R14**, and **C1 = 10 µF**. The oscillation frequency follows the standard astable relation:

```
f ≈ 1.44 / ((R_A + 2·R_B) · C)
```

With the chosen values, the output lands at roughly **1 – 1.5 Hz** — fast enough to register as a deliberate blink, slow enough to read as an alert pattern rather than a flicker.

The 555 output fans out to two loads in parallel: **LED3** through **R8 (1 kΩ)** as the blinking red indicator, and the gate network of the fan MOSFET.

### Fan driver

The fan is switched low-side by **Q7 (IRLZ44N)** — a logic-level N-channel MOSFET chosen for its ability to enhance fully from a ~5 V gate drive and carry the ~0.66 A the 12 V fan draws at full torque without requiring a dedicated gate driver. Two resistors condition the gate node:

- **R18 = 100 Ω** — series gate resistor, limiting peak inrush during the gate-charge transient
- **R19 = 100 kΩ** — gate pull-down to ground, holding the MOSFET off whenever the control line is undriven or floating

The fan sits between VEE (12 V) and Q7's drain; Q7's source ties to ground. When the 555 output goes HIGH, the gate charges through R18 and Q7 turns on. When the 555 goes LOW, R19 drags the gate back to 0 V and the fan turns off within one gate-charge time constant.

## Operating Cases

Three operating points were validated in Multisim by sweeping the potentiometer that stands in for the thermistor. Measurements below were read from on-schematic multimeters in the respective simulation runs.

| Case | `V_sense` | LED1 (yellow) | LED2 (red steady) | LED3 (red blinking) | Fan |
|------|-----------|---------------|-------------------|---------------------|-----|
| Low — safe | ≈ 0 V | ON | OFF | OFF | OFF (≈ 5 µA leakage) |
| Medium — moderate heat | ≈ 4 V | OFF | ON | OFF | OFF (≈ 5 µA leakage) |
| High — alert | ≈ 8 V | OFF | OFF | Blinking at ~1 Hz | ON (≈ 0.66 A) |

The µA leakage current reported in the two OFF states is drain–source leakage of the IRLZ44N combined with the R19 pull-down path — six orders of magnitude below any meaningful fan actuation threshold.

## Design Notes

**Op-amp versus comparator.** An earlier iteration used a general-purpose op-amp as the threshold detector. The op-amp output swing never reached ground cleanly — a residual LOW of a few hundred millivolts left the downstream BJT base biased above V_BE, which held the LED in a dim half-on state through the transition region. Replacing the op-amp with the LM311 comparator and adding an explicit pull-up pulled the LOW rail hard to ground and produced a clean digital transition at the threshold.

**BJT base resistors.** The first wiring pass drove several BJT bases directly from comparator and 555 outputs. The base–emitter junction behaved as a forward-biased diode and sank enough current to disturb the upstream node voltage, which in turn shifted the decision thresholds. Adding base resistors (R6, R7, R11, R15, R16, R17) clamped the base current to a safe range without compromising saturation depth.

**Hardware NOR rationale.** The medium state is logically "not low and not high." A microcontroller or a dedicated 74HC02 would solve this trivially. The discrete implementation chosen here uses parts already in the bill of materials — two BJTs already present for other purposes and a single pull-up resistor — which demonstrates how active-pull / passive-pull topologies realize Boolean functions without any logic IC.

## Simulation

The project targets **Multisim 14.0** or newer. To run:

1. Open `Schematic.ms14` in Multisim.
2. Press **Run** (F5) to start the simulation.
3. Double-click the potentiometer `R8` and use the keyboard slider (default key `A`) to sweep the wiper position. Each position maps one of the three `V_sense` bands listed above.
4. The on-schematic multimeters display `V_sense`, the reference voltages, and the fan current for live verification.

On real hardware, the potentiometer is replaced by a two-terminal NTC thermistor in series with a fixed 10 kΩ resistor to form the sensing divider. The threshold dividers (R1/R4 for V_high, R10/R2 for V_low) can be retuned to match the thermistor's resistance–temperature curve at the desired trip temperatures.

## Future Work

- **Hysteresis.** Adding positive feedback around each LM311 would trade a fixed deadband for immunity to oscillation when `V_sense` lingers near a threshold. A 100 kΩ resistor from the output back to the non-inverting input is the typical starting point.
- **Temperature calibration.** The current thresholds are set by divider arithmetic on VCC. Matching them to real physical temperatures requires characterizing the specific NTC thermistor's β coefficient and nominal resistance, then solving for the corresponding divider voltages.
- **Audible alert.** A small piezoelectric buzzer in parallel with LED3 would add an aural channel to the high-temperature alert without additional logic.
- **Digital path.** Replacing the analog threshold network with an MCU ADC sample + software comparison would open the door to data logging, user-settable thresholds, PWM fan speed control, and remote status reporting — at the cost of the all-discrete pedagogical value the current design holds.
